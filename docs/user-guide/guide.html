DataCentral Pulse
User guide · two roles, one platform

Everything you need to use DataCentral Pulse.

Pulse is where customers tell DataCentral what to build next — and where the DataCentral team turns that feedback into a roadmap. This guide walks through both sides: the customer who sees their own organization, and the DataCentral team member who sees across the whole operation.

Overview

What Pulse is for

DataCentral Pulse is a shared workspace for customer feedback and product planning. Customers describe the outcomes they need; DataCentral reviews that evidence, consolidates related requests into product ideas, and communicates decisions back through a public roadmap and release notes. Every request keeps its original context and a full status history, and nothing a customer writes is ever shared with another customer.

The same platform serves two very different jobs, which is why this guide is organized around the two roles. If you're a customer, you can skip straight to Home & navigation for customers. If you're on the DataCentral team, jump to the full navigation. The access model and AI assistant sections apply to everyone.

Customers get

A clear channel to ask for what they need, visibility into what's planned, and honest status on everything their organization has submitted.

DataCentral gets

One triage queue, evidence-linked product ideas, scoring, a governed roadmap, and release communication — all in one place.

Everyone gets

An AI assistant (in-app, in Slack, or over MCP) that can do anything you're allowed to do — and politely refuses anything you're not.

The big picture

How a piece of feedback travels

A request doesn't disappear into a black box. It moves through a defined lifecycle, and both roles can see the parts of that journey relevant to them. Customers see the request-level status; DataCentral sees — and drives — every stage.

01
Submit

A customer describes a problem or outcome. It becomes a request with an id like DCI-1042.

02
Triage

DataCentral classifies it: start discovery, link to an idea, ask for information, route to support, or close.

03
Consolidate

Related requests across customers become one product idea IDEA-318, keeping each customer's context private.

04
Plan

Product managers score the idea and place it on the roadmap at a horizon: Now, Next, or Later.

05
Release

When shipped, a release REL-### moves ideas to Released and notifies the customers who followed them.

Throughout, ids are meaningful: DCI-#### is a customer request, IDEA-### is a product idea, REL-### is a release. You'll see these everywhere, including in the assistant.

The two roles at a glance

Who sees what

The single most important thing to understand about Pulse is that the same screens serve both roles, but the data behind them is scoped to who you are. A customer and a DataCentral admin can both open “Product ideas”, but the customer sees an empty, access-controlled shell while the admin sees the internal workspace. Access is decided by the server on every request — never by hiding a button.

The access model

How “sees what they're allowed to” actually works

Pulse enforces permissions at the server, on every request, by re-checking your membership and role in the target organization. The navigation you see is the same for everyone; what differs is what the server is willing to return. This section is the reference for exactly who can do what.

The permission tiers

There are six role names, but they form five access tiers: Requester and Viewer behave identically today, so the matrix below groups them into one column.

Customer roles

Company admin Requester Viewer

All three can submit and follow. Only a Company admin can edit or withdraw other people's requests inside their organization. Requester and Viewer behave identically today.

DataCentral roles

Internal contributor Product manager System admin

Contributors read internal work and triage queues. Product managers add publishing power — status changes, scoring, ideas, releases. System admins add user, organization & platform administration.

The anti-enumeration principle

When you're not allowed to see something, Pulse doesn't say “access denied” — it says “not found.” Forbidden and non-existent both return the same result, which means you can't probe for records that exist but aren't yours. This is deliberate, and it has two consequences worth remembering:

Ids can't be guessed. Asking for a DCI-#### or IDEA-### outside your access returns the same response as an id that never existed.

The assistant answers the same way. Ask about an item you can't access and it replies: “That item doesn't exist or you don't have access to it.” — which can mean either.

Getting in

How you sign in

Pulse supports three ways in, and which one you use depends on how you reached it. In every case, the app converts your sign-in into a short-lived session that lasts 12 hours; when it expires, an embedded session re-handshakes silently and a standalone session sends you back through your identity provider.

Most customers

Inside DataCentral

Open Pulse from the DataCentral apps & tools menu; it loads as a panel and performs a signed handshake with the host. You're already who DataCentral says you are, so there's nothing to log into.

Standalone

With your Microsoft account

Opening Pulse directly sends you through Microsoft Entra ID (single sign-on) and back. Your email must already be provisioned as a Pulse user.

Developers

Local demo mode

On a developer's machine with no database configured, Pulse signs in a demo administrator automatically — never available in production.

The embedded sign-in path. The signature (an HMAC the host and Pulse both know) proves the launch is genuine; Pulse never redirects an iframe to a Microsoft login screen.

Users are provisioned in advance. Whether you arrive through DataCentral or Microsoft sign-in, your email must already exist as a Pulse user with at least one organization membership. If it doesn't, Pulse says your account isn't linked yet — ask your DataCentral contact, and a System admin adds you on the Users screen.

Customer walkthrough · 2

Submit a request

This is the heart of Pulse for a customer. Start with the problem, not a solution — DataCentral will assess the right product response. You can reach the form from the Submit a request button in the header, on Home, or by asking the assistant.

  1. Give it a short title

    Up to 140 characters — a scannable summary like “Scheduled report delivery to SharePoint”.

  2. Describe the problem or outcome

    Up to 5,000 characters. Explain what you're trying to achieve, who's affected, and what happens today. Solutions are welcome, but the problem is what matters.

  3. Add impact and context (optional)

    Expand this to add the product area and how much this is hurting — it helps triage prioritize.

  4. Attach screenshots or files (optional)

    Up to 25 MB each, 100 MB total. Never upload credentials, personal data, secrets, or unredacted production data — the form reminds you of this.

  5. Set visibility, then submit

    Choose whether the request is visible to your whole organization or kept private to you, then press Submit request. Drafts autosave on your device as you type.

Check for duplicates first. The assistant automatically searches existing ideas and your organization's requests before it files anything new, so you can add weight to an existing idea instead of creating a duplicate. You can also do this yourself from Browse ideas.

Customer walkthrough · 3

Track your requests

My requests lists everything your organization has submitted. Each request keeps its original context, its status history, and a link to the product idea it fed into. Tabs across the top let you filter by state, and every request carries a status you can read at a glance.

What the statuses mean

Submitted Needs information Linked Routed to support Closed Withdrawn
Submitted
Received and waiting to be triaged.
Needs information
DataCentral has asked a question in the discussion — reply there, and you can still edit while it's in this state.
Linked
Consolidated into a product idea. Follow that idea to track it on the roadmap.
Routed to support
Better handled as a support case; the reference is recorded on the request.
Closed
Resolved or declined — always with a customer-safe explanation.
Withdrawn
You closed it yourself — the one status change a customer can make.

You can edit a request's title and description only while it's Submitted or Needs information; after that, add a comment instead. Only a Company admin can edit or withdraw requests filed by other people in the organization. (The demo data in these screenshots also shows a few roadmap-style labels like “Under review” and “Planned” — those reflect the linked idea's progress.)

DataCentral walkthrough · 2

Triage inbox

Triage is where raw requests become decisions. The inbox shows untriaged and overdue counts and your median triage time; picking a request opens the customer's problem, suggested related ideas, and a decision panel. Reading the queue is open to any DataCentral contributor; acting on it — changing status, assigning owners — requires a Product manager (or System admin).

  1. Pick a request from the queue

    Filter by Untriaged or Assigned to me. Each card shows the id, product area, and current status.

  2. Read the customer's problem

    The center pane shows impact, product area, visibility, and owner, plus product ideas suggested from the title and context.

  3. Add an internal note (optional)

    Capture evidence, constraints, or a decision rationale. Internal notes are never visible to customers.

  4. Choose the next step

    Link to product idea, Request information, Route to support, and Close request each update the customer-visible status and record the decision. Start discovery is internal-only — it signals that work has begun while the customer still sees Submitted.

Bulk triage handles the backlog. Product managers can update owner, tags, and triage-due date for up to 100 requests at once — but only for requests in organizations they're a member of. The assistant asks for explicit confirmation before running a bulk action.

DataCentral walkthrough · 3

Product ideas

Product ideas is the canonical internal workspace: consolidate customer evidence into one idea, score priorities, stage customer-safe wording, and publish deliberate product decisions. The list shows every idea with its internal status, roadmap horizon, evidence count, and score.

Internal idea statuses

Discovery Candidate Planned In progress Released

Editing a published idea automatically demotes it to Staged until you deliberately re-publish — so customer-facing wording never changes by accident. Publishing requires the wording to be confirmed customer-safe.

Once published, the two early stages are renamed for customers: Discovery appears as Under review and Candidate as Considering. Planned, In progress, and Released keep their names.

Consolidate

Link customer requests (DCI-####) to an idea as evidence, or merge duplicate ideas into a survivor.

Score

Record an impact/reach/alignment/commercial/urgency snapshot; the score is computed server-side from the configured weights.

Publish

Place the idea on the roadmap and publish customer-safe wording — a Product-manager action requiring explicit confirmation.

DataCentral walkthrough · 4

Roadmap & releases

The Roadmap is the customer-facing view you place ideas onto; Releases is how you communicate what shipped. A release bundles one or more ideas, and publishing it is a high-blast-radius action: it cascades the bundled ideas to Released and notifies every customer who followed them, by email and in-app.

Placing on the roadmap

Product managers set an idea's horizon — Now, Next, or Later — with an optional target quarter and confidence. Roadmap items combine related requests while keeping each customer's context private.

Publishing a release

Draft with a title, date, summary, and bundled ideas (REL-###). Publishing notifies followers — so the assistant and the UI both ask you to confirm before it goes out.

Publishing is deliberate, not accidental. Four actions require explicit confirmation before they run: publish_release, publish_idea, merge_ideas, and bulk_triage. In the assistant, that means it explains the effect and waits for your “yes”.

DataCentral walkthrough · 5

Analytics & administration

Two more areas round out the DataCentral experience. Analytics is available to any contributor; the administration screens — Users, Companies, Settings, and the Audit log — are restricted to System admins. (The Authentication screen is an informational configuration preview, not a live control.)

Managing users & access

The Users screen is where System admins provision people and grant access. The model is many-to-many: one identity, any number of explicit company memberships, each with its own role. Internal employees are not global — you assign each person only to the customer companies they support.

Saving a user replaces their entire membership set. The editor sends the complete list of companies you've checked — any membership you leave out is deactivated. When editing over the assistant, always list the user's existing memberships first and round-trip them, so you don't accidentally remove access.

For everyone

The AI assistant

Every signed-in user has an assistant that can do anything they're allowed to do — file a request, check a status, triage a queue, publish a release — using the exact same permission checks as the rest of the app. It's available in three places. The in-app panel and Slack share one conversation history (kept against your account); an MCP client keeps its own history on its side. All three enforce identical permissions.

In the app

A floating panel on every page. Type a message, or use the 🎤 microphone to dictate (in supported browsers). Press Enter to send, Shift+Enter for a new line; an empty Enter after a question means “yes”.

In Slack

DM the pulse app (it replies inline) or @mention it in a channel (it replies in a thread). It identifies you by your Slack email — which must match your Pulse account — and shows an ⏳ reaction while it works.

Over MCP

Connect a compatible client (like Claude) to Pulse's MCP endpoint. A one-time browser sign-in authorizes it, then the same tools are available — still scoped to your permissions.

What to ask it

As a customer
  • “Submit a request: exports to Excel time out for large orders”
  • “What's the status of DCI-1051?”
  • “Show me my open requests”
  • “Is there already an idea about export performance?”
  • “Follow IDEA-318 and mark it as solving our need”
  • “What's on the roadmap for Next?”
As DataCentral staff
  • “Show the triage queue for ORG-014, only Submitted”
  • “Link DCI-1051 to IDEA-318 as evidence”
  • ”Score IDEA-318: impact 4, reach 3, urgency 5…”
  • “Merge IDEA-322 into IDEA-318”
  • “Publish the customer wording for IDEA-318”
  • “How many open requests do we have, and our average first response?”

The assistant can't exceed your permissions. In the app it only shows the tools your role allows; over MCP every tool is offered but the server re-checks each call. Either way the boundary is the same — ask it to do something outside your access and it answers exactly as the rest of the app does: ”That item doesn't exist or you don't have access to it.” Destructive actions always ask you to confirm first.

Assistant model

A defining property: the assistant in the app, the Slack bot, and an MCP client are three doors into the same tool registry, and none of them can bypass the permission layer. Pulse runs as a single instance (the Slack connection and short-lived caches assume it), with always-on hosting.

Connecting over MCP

Pulse is its own OAuth 2.1 authorization server, so a client like Claude can connect securely without any shared secret. The flow reuses your normal Pulse sign-in for the human consent step.

%%{init: {'theme':'neutral'}}%%
sequenceDiagram
    autonumber
    participant C as MCP client
    participant M as Pulse /mcp
    participant O as Pulse OAuth server
    participant U as You (browser)
    C->>M: Connect (no token yet)
    M-->>C: 401 + where to authorize
    C->>O: Register, then open authorize URL
    O->>U: Sign in & consent (normal Pulse login)
    U-->>O: Allow
    O-->>C: Authorization code
    C->>O: Exchange code for tokens
    O-->>C: Access token (1h) + refresh token (60d)
    C->>M: Call tools with the access token
    M-->>C: Results — scoped to your permissions
Tokens are short-lived and rotate: a one-hour access token, a sixty-day refresh token, single-use authorization codes, and a ten-minute consent window. Every tool call re-verifies that your account is still active.

Glossary

Membership
A link between one person and one organization, carrying a role. Access is evaluated per membership.
Product idea
A canonical record consolidating related requests from multiple customers, kept private per customer.
Horizon
A roadmap band — Now, Next, or Later — expressing current delivery intent.
Triage
Classifying an incoming request into the right next step.
Anti-enumeration
Returning “not found” for both forbidden and non-existent items, so records can't be probed.
MCP
The protocol that lets an external AI client use Pulse's tools securely, under your permissions.
